Wildfires

Wildfires are uncontrolled, unplanned, and unpredictable fires that frequently burn in forested or grassy
areas. Although wildfires have been a part of nature for hundreds of millions of years, climate change has
caused them to become more severe, long-lasting, and frequent. Recent wildfires, like the one in Hawaii in
2023, where over a thousand people have vanished are evidence of this. With approximately 37 million hectares
of land burned in the US and 5.3 million hectares of land burned in Canada so far in 2023, the total area
burned is more than 15 times bigger than the season's 10-year average. Every year the spread of these fires
has devastating effects on infrastructure in communities around the world, forcing millions from their homes
and resulting in billions of dollars in damages. Millions of people are forced out of their homes each year
as a result of the destruction these flames do to infrastructure in communities throughout the world. In
addition to destroying essential infrastructure, wildfire smoke contains a variety of dangerous air pollutants,
including lead, ozone, nitrogen dioxide, and aromatic hydrocarbons. Large amounts of carbon dioxide and other
greenhouse gasses are also released into the atmosphere, which furthers the effects of climate change. Given
the current environmental climate, it is crucial that the general public has a robust understanding of the
dangers that wildfires pose to their communities, the apparent health effects that they pose, and an understanding
of what do in the case of a wildfire.
